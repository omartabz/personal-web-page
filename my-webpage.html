Favourite City

Tokyo,Japan

Japan has a long and fascinating history, with traditions that are still practiced today. You can visit ancient temples and shrines, stroll through serene gardens, or attend a traditional tea ceremony. Japan is home to stunning natural landscapes, from snow-capped mountains and serene bamboo forests to pristine beaches and vibrant coral reefs. You can hike in the Japanese Alps, go skiing in Hokkaido, or relax on a beach in Okinawa. Lastly the variety of food ranges from sushi, sashimi, tempura, ramen, and many other dishes.

Foreign Language: Japanese

The most widely spoken language in Japan is Japanese , called Nihongo (日本語) by the indigenous people. here are actually several dialects of Japanese spoken throughout the country, but the dialect of Tokyo is considered the standard form. In addition to Japanese, there are a couple of other minority languages spoken in Japan such as Ryukyuan and Ainu.

Historic places in Johannesburg

Constitutional Hill

Johannesburg's Constitution Hill is a powerful place that fuses South Africa's harsh past with its hopeful future. Once a notorious prison complex, it now houses the country's Constitutional Court, symbolizing the triumph of justice. Exhibits within the old fort walls tell the stories of those incarcerated there, including Nelson Mandela, while the court itself safeguards the hard-won human rights of all South Africans.

Mandela House

Mandela House, also known as the Nelson Mandela National Museum, is a historic site in Soweto, South Africa. It's the modest brick house where Nelson Mandela lived from 1946 to 1962. Today, it's a museum showcasing his life and fight against apartheid. The house itself has some original furnishings and even bullet holes from past attacks. Mandela House serves as a powerful symbol of Mandela's legacy and the struggle for equality in South Africa.

Walter Sisulu square

Walter Sisulu Square in Kliptown, Soweto is a place steeped in history. It's where the Freedom Charter, a pivotal document outlining a vision for a non-racial South Africa, was adopted in 1955. Today, the square is a national heritage site with an open-air museum showcasing the Charter's creation. While some descriptions mention it also having entertainment options, its historical significance remains the top draw.
